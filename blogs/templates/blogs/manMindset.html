The Social Stigma Against Men's Mental Health


Attention to men's mental issues has increased substantially in the last few years as social media influencers, content creators, activists complemented by experts in criminology, psychology, and education have successfully agitated the society. However, as opposed to women, men are three times more likely to perpetrate suicide in Australia, 3.5 times more likely in the US, and more than four times in Russia and Argentina.

Nevertheless, psychiatric surveys routinely indicate that men have a significantly lower prevalence than women of common mental disorders, at a ratio of approximately 1:2. The discrepancy between men's low recorded rates of mental illnesses and high rates of suicide are the most compelling evidence that there is indeed a "silent crisis" in men's mental health that is not being sufficiently addressed.





Researchers Nolen-Hoeksma and Girgus have implied that men are known to blunt the reporting of possible mental distress symptoms because they are accustomed to believing that these symptoms are inconsistent with the dominant notions of masculinity. Hence, in contrast to women, who "act-in" in case of psychological suffering, men tend to "act-out." This "acting-out" is often in the form of alcohol and drug abuse, risk-taking, weak impulse control, and increased anger and irritability. These behaviours are considered to be more consistent with society's notions of manliness, in place of crying or even talking about their feelings.

However, the standard interpretation that men's underutilization of mental health resources is due to the man's stubbornness can be reasonably construed as "victim-blaming." We have chosen to ignore the villainous role played by the societal deterrents such as the typical "log kya kaheingein?", "be a man", "boys don't cry'' mentality which has caused substantial damage to not only an ailing man's will to seek help but also our public resource system's duty to provide service to said men. Numerous studies have inferred that healthcare workers are both less likely to diagnose mental illness in men than women & are less likely to act upon mental illness in men even on detection.

“Mental illness is nothing to be ashamed of, but stigma and bias shame us all.”
– Bill Clinton

Let us look at some common risk factors now:

1. Occupation-related stress is one of the leading causes of depression and anxiety in men. Research indicates that unemployment can be a continuous cause for stress, whereas being laid-off can be an acute stressor. This is mainly because work, i.e. "winning bread for the family", has been made the domain from which a man derives his self-worth. Studies have proven that low job security and dispute that resulted from competing family and work responsibilities were predictive of extreme mental distress in men. In contrast to this, moderate levels of pressure in the work environment were found to alleviate anxiety and depression in men, signifying the substantial role of 'employment' regarding men's mental health.



2. Adverse childhood experiences are also a causative factor of mental trauma in men. Regardless of how unpleasant it sounds, childhood abuse is abundantly prevalent in both boys and girls. However, some forms of abuse are likelier to happen to girls than boys, and vice versa. While more girls than boys are victims of childhood sexual abuse, boys fall prey to more physical abuse than girls. As per extensive research, abused girls tend to display conventional mental health symptoms, such as low self-esteem, self-harm and disordered eating, which, although requiring much care, are comparatively easier to diagnose and treat. On the contrary, as previously discussed, boys display a constellation of delinquent behaviours, such as violence, recreational drug use, smoking, etc., lead to a disciplinary response. Being unable to attribute this disruptive behaviour to mental unhealthiness, officials at educational centres and elsewhere punish the child, allowing the illness or trauma to go untreated.

3. Numerous contemporary researches have diagnosed a mostly undetected disease, known as Paternal Postpartum Depression (PPD), which affects 10.4% of fathers. Various factors, such as a genetic history of depression, marital friction, accidental pregnancy, and poverty, contribute to PPD. Hormonal changes preceding the birth of the child are also greatly contributive to exacerbating PPD. For example, to make a new father more sympathetic and less aggressive to the sound of his crying baby, the body undergoes momentous changes such as a decline in the level of testosterone. However, low testosterone levels may cause depression and impair father-infant bonding, leading to a father's worsening mood.



The above-discussed factors are a mere preview into the deeper labyrinth of the complications involved with men's mental health. Society's deficit-based models need to be abolished, and concrete reforms need to be initialized and implemented, especially regarding the development of 'men-friendly' help centres. For many men, especially if you are suffering from depression, reaching out for help can seem incredibly overwhelming.

The ability to develop and cherish close relationships is a vital step to becoming a friend of your mind. If you feel that you do not have anyone to turn to, remember that you can quickly build new relationships on a planet this populated if you try hard enough. The plethora of resources available online, too, are specifically designed to cater to your needs. No one is immune to the distinctions of the human psyche, so do not let anyone make you feel that your emotions are less valid.
