Humanity Against Racismh


After the name was patented in 1971, it took us about 30 years to realize what was wrong with the name 'Fair & Lovely' - this is the world we are living in, where the colour of the skin that our ancestors passed onto us is considered "unnatural", thus leading to "necessary corrective measures"; and this is a mere gust of wind, a part of a more significant storm - which has shaken the core of human existence and is gradually hollowing our roots.





Racism, a relatively short term to embody all that it is characterized by, is the prejudice by an individual, community, or institution against a person or people based on their membership of a particular racial or ethnic group, typically one that is considered a minority or marginalized. This antagonism, based on the belief that humans may be divided into separate and exclusive biological entities called "races," depends on establishing a causal link between inherited physical traits and personality traits, intellect, morality, and other cultural and behavioural features.

Racism, xenophobia, etc., have been prevalent for a very long time now. From the urban settings of colonized India to the Auschwitz-Birkenau concentration camps in Nazi-occupied Poland and the lush fields of South Africa which were set aside for the "white minority" during the apartheid era, the discrimination based on a person's membership in a "racial collective nation" has been razing the purity of the entire essence of humankind. However, the notion that racism is seated comfortably on the wealthier's silk sheets can seriously undermine the graveness it has adopted. It is not only ravaging the lives of the rural folk in India, as evident by the unsubtle and perpetual friction between specific communities, but has also been able to creep into the innocent lives of the citizens of the future, as young students have not been able to shield themselves from racial slurs and marginalization and the consequent trauma involved with not being able to fit in with the other students.

“No one is born hating another person because of the color of his skin, or his background, or his religion. People must learn to hate, and if they can learn to hate, they can be taught to love, for love comes more naturally to the human heart than its opposite.”
– Nelson Mandela

However, what is it that causes people to be and continue being racist? All of this unwarranted hate and violence can be attributed to people's receptiveness to whatever persists in society. Parents do not actively instil racist notions in their child's minds. The child sees what the environment is nurturing around him and how others behave. This air around them is what racism stems from, not some inherent hatred or "evil". Categorization of people into sects, primarily based on whom we identify ourselves with, is not unnatural. How we choose to define our groups makes us a friend or foe of "progress" - and the marginalization of the other groups makes someone racist if the groups are defined based on the constituent members' race.



To aptly understand to what degree our environment affects our mindset imperceptibly, a study at Tufts University found that the mere body language of white and black actors interacting in a TV show was sufficient for the viewers to test higher for implicit bias, even when the TV was on mute.

Further, although it is sufficiently evident that the youth are more egalitarian and progressive, purging racist ideologies is more challenging because it is on their implicit level of bias. The fear that people of different races will not be able to coexist thrivingly and will rob them off of employment opportunities and their sense of security is what drives racism at the core. Generalizing based on one or two negative experiences, stereotyping, fueled with rumours and ignorance, exacerbates the fear, leading to violence. The death of George Floyd on May 25th, 2020, is a classic, disheartening example of acts of violence perpetrated against people of colour by the apparent "racially supreme".

Additionally, the number of anti-Asian hate incidents, including shunning, slurs and physical attacks, in 2020-21 is substantially more than it used to be, and a disproportionate number of attacks have been directed at women. Lastly, what structures racist groups is our leaders' ignorance and the state of denial that our policymakers are seemingly living in. Failing to acknowledge racism in all its forms, in the entertainment industry, healthcare sectors, administration positions, or employment divisions, ending racism can also be initiated if we acknowledge that it is there. Gaslighting people who try to speak up, discrediting them, are the more subtle, unstructured forms of racism, which are not acknowledged by our leaders. Because such instances are not always greeted with opprobrium, it conveys to them that these feelings and their consequent actions are acceptable.

It cannot be denied that there have reforms to combat the stigma involved with interracial marriages and abolish discrimination laws. However, our interpersonal biases, our implicit prejudices, cannot be shaken with a new amendment. Racism is not going to go away with the passing of generations. The media is actively playing its role, but only individual enlightenment can hasten our amelioration from the consequences of having prejudices regarding a varied ancestry, differing beliefs, or more or less melanin in their skins than the perceived majority.
