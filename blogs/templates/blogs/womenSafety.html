Women’s Safety


Women comprise roughly 48% of the Indian population. If about half the population lives under constant fear and oppression, can India really think of progress?

Even after 73 years of Independence, our country's women do not enjoy freedom - they still feel gravely unsafe in their bodies. It is the collective responsibility of society, rather than just the government, to ensure our women's safety, and it is high time that we take collective action against this menace. As grossly misinterpreted sometimes, women's safety is not restricted to "making our cities safer for women," as many policymakers have not failed to promise in their rallies.

The establishment of safe spaces for reporting crimes and recuperating from them, in tandem with the liberation of women from the mental trauma involved due to fear or insecurity, also fall within the ambit of 'women's safety.'





According to the National Crimes Records Bureau (NCRB), India recorded 32033 rape cases in 2019 - that is 88 rape cases every day. A woman falls prey to a crime every minute. A woman is raped every 15 minutes and murdered every 35 minutes in India. With this generation being the most "woke" of all, as we so proudly claim on our social media handles, with impeccable awareness of all social evils, why is it that the vulnerability of a woman or even a little girl today has increased up to 44% in the last ten years? And these numbers are merely the 'recorded' cases.

According to numerous studies in this field, it is very likely that countless women across the country are too oppressed and afraid to report an offender due to the degree of her abuse and the stigmatization involved with the subject.

Let's look at some (of the many, unfortunately) causes why a female's life's substantial proportion is wasted in trying to infer whether "is this city really safe, though?"

1. Delay in decision making, and execution of punishments

The 2012 gang rape and murder case of 23-year-old Jyoti Singh "Nirbhaya" was deliberated for eight years before four of the convicted were finally executed on 20th March 2020. Such prolonged execution of justice is one of the leading factors of continued female abuse. Although the government introduced fast-track courts after a gruesome assault on a student in a Delhi bus shocked the country in 2012, to deal with sensitive cases like these. However, it still did not deter the miscreants.

Not only does prolonged justice take away the public's faith in the judiciary, but it also eases the pressure on would-be perpetrators. Furthermore, the absolute anguish that the victim and their families go through is exacerbated every time the topic is up for deliberation. The agony involved with having to relive the worst possible day of your life and having to directly, indirectly face those responsible, complemented by the quips of our very opinionated society, are unimaginable.

2. Misogynistic portrayal of women by the entertainment industry

The constant intake of media content in which women are portrayed as weak or entirely dependent upon their male counterparts lead to a subtle altering of the viewer's mindset. Being exposed to such material regularly can hurt their perception of women, misconstruing them as "weak" and "dependent."

It cannot be denied that the entertainment industry has come a long way to cast or portray women in empowering roles. Movies like Mardaani, Kahaani, Neerja, Mission Mangal, and Disney's Moana, Mulan, etc., are testaments to this. Despite this rising wave of change in the entertainment industry ameliorating women's depiction, there is still a long way to go from here to complete, total emancipation of women from roles that lead to their objectification.



3. Usage of social media without a proper understanding of its drawbacks

It is not very challenging to create a fake identity on social media these days, and thanks to the enhanced social connectivity blessing our generation, establishing contact with strangers is notably convenient.

Due to these sorts of evils being rampant online, a deficit of thorough knowledge on online resources' usage, exchanging passwords with "friends", sharing exploitable data with other anonymous users, and uploading sensitive information to the cloud, etc., can cost one dearly.

There have been many instances of social media being a double edged sword, one should always be careful when using social media as although they brought wonderful gifts to us, none should forget the mischiefs that can be committed too.

4. Lack of proper training to health-care providers and law enforcement officials

Some law enforcers and professional caregivers fail to care for a victim of abuse with an added sense of sympathy and kindness. Some tend to show disbelief at the woman's report. This deters the woman's will to report abuse, in most trapping her in the cycle of abuse, especially in cases of domestic violence.



To conclude, having identified some causes of continued violence against women, the need for a radical approach when combatting these evils is a necessity. Article 21 of the Indian Constitution mentions the right to life as the basic fundamental right; this includes the right to live with dignity. Activists, law-enforcers, and even the general public have shown widespread unrest and a diminishing tolerance regarding the lack of stringency about matters like these.

Social media is flooded with content regarding women's safety, resources for help, hotlines, counselling, and a large proportion of netizens never fail to join hands to combat injustice. A plethora of private organizations for healing and therapy are being set up, especially for women, as the people have come to realize the lack of momentum towards abolishing any wrongdoing towards our country's women. The government, too, is actively working towards the liberation of women from fear and insecurity. Given our country's population, it is very likely that these reforms may take years to set in. Still, perseverance, and zero tolerance towards any female abuse, is the key to our country's progress.

This problem is deep-rooted and multi-dimensional, and formulating extensive laws alone cannot solve this issue. The attitude of society towards women needs to be changed, which is possible only through heightened awareness and action by individuals. One should take it as their responsibility to treat everyone with the utmost regard, especially women who have lost their sense of security, no matter how big or little it may be in your opinion. Treat every woman with care and implore them to seek help. A few words of kindness and sympathy may not completely heal her wounds, but the next time she heads out, maybe she can wear her scars with renewed pride and a sense of self at the marvel that she is!
