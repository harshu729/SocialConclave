Unemployment


The global employment rate was no better before the COVID-19 pandemic, yet it has worsened, increasing the unemployment rate by 1.1 percentage points to 6.5 percentage which has left more than 33 million people unemployed, taking the global total to 220 million in 2020. This increase is far larger than that observed during the global financial crisis of 2008, one of the worst economic disasters of modern times, when the increase in employment rate was 0.6%.

In contrast to the global financial crisis of 2008, the COVID-19 crisis has affected labour markets worldwide, leading to greater job losses and unemployment spikes everywhere including low and middle income countries, which weren't hit as hard during the global financial crisis. The International Labour Organisation estimated that by June 30, COVID-19 eliminated 277 billion hours of paid work, the equivalent of 400 million full-time jobs.





When pondering upon the term unemployment, one generally believes that it refers to a person who was unable to get a job post reaching a particular age, but that isn’t the case. When calculating the Labour Force Participation Rate which is measured by diving the total labour force by the working age population, it must be realised that the working age includes those aged 15 and above, therefore, it also accounts for those people who are unemployed simply because they are not wishing to be employed for a myriad of reasons. The global average Labour force participation rate today stands at 61%.

According to CMIE (Centre for Monitoring Indian Economy), the unemployment rate in India is 7% (7% in urban areas and 7.1% in rural areas). The younger popu-lous in India has a high unemployment rate, with almost half the demography falling under the category. The unemployment rate in the age group of 20-24 is 46.4%. Unemployment has several adverse impacts on a person including disillusionment, frustration, and dissatisfaction. This gives rise to cynicism and dissipates the energy of the youth towards destructive directions.

Thus, it isn’t impossible to see why they wouldn’t resort to crime, violence, anti-social activities or even worse, seek the easier path to achieve the much sought-after social status. But it doesn’t just stop there, suicidal tendencies are also on an increase due to the social humiliation and deprivation that the unemployed status attracts from society.

"There is no such thing as an acceptable level of unemployment, because hunger is not acceptable, poverty is not acceptable, poor health is not acceptable, and a ruined life is not acceptable."
~Hubert H. Humphrey

In order to tackle the over looming issue of unemployment it is imperative that we try to understand its root causes, some of which are as follows:

1. Slow rate of economic growth: Okun's law explains the inverse relationship between the growth rate and the unemployment rate. A slow economic process rate indicates that not enough jobs are being created hire the workers able and willing to work.

2. Increase in population growth rate: India has a population of 1.38 billion and a population growth rate of almost 1%. The rising population has a serious implication on various socio-economic factors. The population has crossed the optimum limit in India and has become a liability. So, the problem of overpopulation in India has proved to be a big obstacle in the success of economic planning and development. A rapidly increasing population reduces income, savings, and investment. Thus, capital formation is retarded thus creating a burden on the number of jobs that can actually be created in an economy with its limited resources., thereby increasing unemployment. Overpopulation also creates more competition for employment.

3. Over-dependence on technology: An increase in the pace of technological change can have two profound side effects in the labour market. It can increase the rate and therefore the average duration of unemployment. Because firms might not consider it cost-effective to retrain some kinds of workers to keep up with change, notably the less-educated and the older employees, these workers could also be jobless for long periods, with a number of them perhaps never working again. But technology both eliminates and creates jobs. Generally, it destroys low wages, low productivity jobs, but it also creates jobs that are more productive, high-skill, and better paid. Advancements in technology and the advent of digitisation mean that firms have the ability to produce goods with fewer workers, thus reducing the blue-collar worker force but at the same time, the increased productivity in manufactured goods mean that the relative cost falls whilst creating a demand in the service sector.

4. Caste system: In India, the caste system is predominant and is influential in the con-text of getting employed. It is evident that employment opportunities are denied to particular castes in certain regions and the ideology of non-discriminatory employ-ment on the basis of skill and ability is not upheld. Thus, there is an increase in un-employment rates for lack of fair opportunities.



5. Skill gap: Skill gap occurs when the demand and supply sides of the employment market don't match. It is the persistent disconnect existing between the abilities needed by a corporation and therefore the present capabilities of its workforce. The skill gap in India remains a significant issue impeding the growth potentials of the economy. The International Labour Organisation (ILO) believes that India is staring at a 29 million skill-deficit by 2030. Accenture projects that the skill gap in India will cause a loss of $1.97 trillion in terms of gross domestic product promised by investment in intelligent technologies over the 10 years if it is not controlled.

Many factors are attributed to causing the skill gap in India but the primary cause of this problem is the failure of the educational system. It encourages students to think that talent is measured by the grades of the students. Practical knowledge and appli-cation of whatever they learn within the confines of a classroom are necessary, this is another factor that is overlooked in the educational system. Most students are unable to process and understand the concept of what is being taught in class and resort to rote learning for examinations. Bridging the skills gap between work and education is imperative to sustain the productivity and growth of the economy.

But that doesn’t mean that the authorities have been oblivious to this issue. Till date about 2 crore migrant workers are provided Rs.5000 crores directly in their bank accounts from building and other construction workers ‘cess fund’ being maintained by various states. In order to resolve the complaints of migrant workers during lockdown, the Ministry of Labour and Employment had initiated 20 Control Rooms all over the country and over 15000 grievances of the workers were resolved through these Control Rooms.



Another aspect that the government is scrutinising is using the infrastructure of skill development ministry’s ITIs to impart skills to workers to prepare them for job op-portunities within the pandemic-affected world. The skill development ministry, led by Mahendra Nath Pandey, is already holding consultations with various sector skill councils to enhance the employability of the country’s workforce.

Amidst the Covid recovery phase, the government launched a new scheme called Atma Nirbhar Bharat Rozgar Yojana to incentivise creation of new employment op-portunities. Under this scheme the central government would provide subsidy for two years in respect of new eligible employees after 1st October 2020. Several initiatives were taken by the government to curb unemployment like integrated Rural Development Programme, Aatmanirbhar Bharat Abhiyan, Robust and Resilient Agricultural Sector, Strong and Diversified Industrial and Infrastructural Base.

Many countries have adopted different strategies to try to mitigate the worst of the effects. The key elements that are most required in this situation are models for man-aging labour market shocks, which will not only provide effective protection in the short run but also offer extended support where the shock persists or reoccurs (as seems to be the case with Covid-19). Ultimately, successful policy approaches will need to be well suited for enabling job creation on a large scale.
